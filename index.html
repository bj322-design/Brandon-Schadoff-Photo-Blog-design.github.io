Artist: Jo Spence and Rosy Martin

Time Period: 1989

This series by Jo Spence and Rosy Martin named, “Libido Uprising”, features two images: one of Spence armed with a hoover, and another of her wearing fishnet stockings and heels with the vacuum tube coiled up her leg. The first image is symbolizing the role of a housewife while the second is symbolizing the role of a seductress.

The photographer has made several technical choices:

Framing: Each image is individually framed, isolating the two roles and emphasizing their distinctness.

Focus: The focus is on Spence and the hoover in both images, drawing attention to the roles she is performing.

POV: The point of view is straightforward, allowing the viewer to directly engage with the images.

Shapes: The shapes of the hoover and Spence’s body in different poses contribute to the narrative of each image.

Use of Light: The images are brightly colored, creating a stark contrast with the dark background and highlighting the subjects.

Use of Colour: The use of colour further emphasizes the contrast between the two roles Spence is portraying.

What unites these images as a series is the theme of challenging female stereotypes. Both images depict the same individual, Spence, performing two clichéd roles that women are often expected to fulfill: the housewife and the seductress. This juxtaposition of roles in the series provides a critique of societal expectations and norms. The use of the same prop, the hoover, in both images also unifies them, serving as a symbol of domesticity that is both embraced and subverted. The series is a powerful commentary on gender roles and societal expectations.

Week 5: Edward Burtnsky Exibition (Saatchi Gallery)

Artist: Edward Burtnsky

This series offers a profound exploration of the intersection between natural landscapes and human intervention.

The first image, "Nickel Tailings #34 & #35, Sudbury, Ontario, Canada, 1996", depicts a vibrant red river of oxidized liquid waste, a stark reminder of the environmental impact of mining activities.

The second image, "Morenci Mine #2, Clifton, Arizona, USA, 2012", provides an aerial perspective of a copper mine. The intricate patterns and textures, along with the varying colors, highlight the scale of human extraction activities.

The final image, "Magado Crater #1, Kenya, 2017", showcases a multi colored lake in a crater, a natural formation used by local tribes for cattle watering and soda ash harvesting.









The lake's hues, attributed to microbial life attracted to its salty water, add a biological dimension to the series. Collectively, these images, and the series, form a compelling narrative about the complex relationship between humanity and the Earth's resources.

They invite viewers to reflect on the environmental consequences of our actions and the delicate balance that exists between exploitation and sustainability.









He used leading lines and patterns of shapes to help bring these images, and thus his message to life. I would consider these good images due to their ability to show beauty hidden in plain view all this time. He offered a different perspective to stunning landscapes that most never see.

I think the effect of seeing several images together as a series is striking. When you see one it is interesting. Though, when you see them all together it is breathtaking.

The written information changes your understanding of the photos due to the photos’ more abstract nature. Because it’s not a common angle these shots are coming from, it necessitates the context to truly understand and appreciate these images more.

Week 8: Recreate The Photo

Artist: Daido Moriyama

The image employs high contrast lighting, with stark shadows and bright highlights that create a dramatic and intense visual effect. The light source appears to be positioned to the left of the subject and seems to be not diffused. This source of light is casting a deep shadow that accentuate the form and texture of the subject (Hat).

Light is used effectively to emphasize the contours and shapes within the image, particularly highlighting the curves and depressions on the hat. The relation between light and shadow adds depth, creating a three dimensional effect in the two dimensional medium of photography.

The mood is mysterious and intense due to the pronounced shadows and highlights; it evokes a sense of intrigue as viewers are drawn into exploring details revealed by light. It does this with a low aperture of possibly f2 f4, creating a large bokeh effect around the hat. In addition, his mood is helped by his strategic use of vignette around the whole image. This vignette effect on 35mm film can be achieved through dodge and burn in the darkroom

Concretely, this is an image of a hat with prominent lighting casting significant shadows which accentuate its shape.

Abstractly, this photo could be interpreted as an exploration of form and texture brought out through dramatic lighting; it transcends its subject of a hat to become an artwork focusing on visual elements like contrast, shape, and form. It captures the signature style of Daido Moriyama that transforms ordinary subjects into visually striking compositions in a perspective most would normally not see or focus on.









My process in recreating the photo of a hat by Daido Moriyama was a little challenging. Not having the same hat as he used to challenge it a little bit. In addition, from the looks of the original photo, Moriyama used natural lighting, and as such I had to wait for the perfect time of day in which the light, coming into my room, hit the hat at a similar angle. Though, when I took this photo, I had to take it from the other direction due to the direction of light coming in.

I used a similar technique of dodge and burn that the Moriyama used, though digitally rather than in a dark room. I tried to make the main focus of the image only the hat and stray from a lot of distracting details similar to his techniques.
